Three Usability Mistakes

1.Tiny Clickable Areas

    I’ve always clicked the wrong links or can’t click on the right link I want because the hyperlinks are too small. Hyperlinks are designed to be clicked, so to make them usable, it makes sense to ensure that they’re easy to click. Our hand movement with the mouse isn’t very precise, so designers must ensure that the users have a large clickable area.
    If you can’t leave much space for every single hyperlink or make it big, one way you can do is to increase the padding of it. Even if it’s small, the user has low probability to click on the wrong links.

2.No Way to Get In Touch

    User engagement is important if you want to build a successful websites and social apps. Quickly answering user’s question and solving their problems mean that you have good customer service and you care about your users. As a result, the probalitiy that you users appreciate your products and recommend to others will increase. This is an era of information exchange and social media. Almost everyone communicate to others and people are inclined to have interaction with others. Your website will be out of date and abondoned by the users if there is little space for users to get in touch with each other or get answered.

3.Long Registration Forms

    Registration forms are barriers. They are barriers because it takes effort to fill them in, and the task itself is no fun. I will have anxiety and impatience if the website I visit have a long registration and a lot of text to fill.To solve this, we can shrink this barrier by making the registration process as short as possible. Delete those information that you don’t need and serves little important. Show only what the users truly need to register and the rest can be filled in later.


10 Usability in Web Design

1. 2-Second-Rule

    People will feel anxious and impatient if I wait too long for website response. A loose principle is that a user shouldn't wait more than 2 seconds for a system response. Remember that the less time user have to wait, the better is the user experience.

2. Permit easy reversal of actions.

    Experienced users strongly desire the sense that they are in charge of the interface and that the interface responds to their actions. They don’t want surprises or changes in familiar behavior, and they are annoyed by tedious data-entry sequences, difficulty in obtaining necessary information, and inability to produce their desired result.

3.Satisficing

    Web users don’t prefer optimal ways to find the information they’re looking for. They aren’t interested in the most reasonable and sound solution to their problem. Instead they permanently scan for quick solutions which are “good enough”. Applied to Web, satisficing describes exactly this approach: users settle with a solution to a problem that is “good enough” — even if alternative solutions can better fulfill their requirements in a long run.

4. 3-Click-Rule

    Peo are inclined to stop using the site if they can't find the information they need within 3 mouse clicks. 3 clicks is a little bit exaggerated, but it points out that users want to find the information they need as soon as possible. To conclude, designer must make sure that the websites have clear navigation , logical structure and easy-to-follow site hierarchy.

5. You don't use too small font sizes

    I have long passages in which words are small and hard to read. People will probability close your website if they find it very hard to look for information. Don't use small font sizes and make your page easy to read.

6. You make it easy to contact you

    Maybe some website creator don't want to be contacted by visitor, but this may leads to user loss because they want to contact you and they can't find any contact information. If you seek better development of your websites, leave easy-to-find contact information and interact with users.

7. You have at most one animation per page

    Blinking images can attract user's eyeballs and increase content richness. But too many blinking images will distract users from reading your content and make them anoyed. Use at most one animation per page.

8. Eye-Tracking

    Eye tracking is the process of measuring either the point of gaze ( “where we are looking”) or the motion of an eye relative to the head. Using eye-tracking studies can help to estimate how comfortable users are with your website and how they understand the structure and system behind it.

9. Readability

    Readability describes the degree to which the user can understand the content of your website, based on the complexity of sentences and the difficulty of vocabulary. Indexes for readability of your website are supposed to be based on the age or knowledge level of your users. You should create simple and readable content if your users are mostly teenagers.

10. Granularity

    Granularity is the degree to which a large, usually complex data set or information has been broken down into smaller units. Granularity is crucial when you display content that is long, difficult and full of terminology. People feel impatient and hard to continue if your content is a whole page where all things are bundled together. Spliting complex data or information into smaller units can bring ease and readability to readers


5 Key Principles of Good Website Usability

1. Availability and Accessibility

    If people try to access your website and it doesn’t work — for whatever reason –your website becomes worthless.
    Some basic rules of availability and accessibility:
        Server uptime: Invest in good hosting and ensure your server is stable and fast.
        Broken links: Double check there are no dead links.
        Mobile responsiveness： Make sure your site can handle different screen sizes and slow connections.

2. Clarity

    The core of Usability is clarity. If you distract or confuse your visitors, they will need more time to find what they came for. Either way, they will not experience your website and chances are that they leave with no intention of coming back.     It is your job to help visitors reach their goals as quickly as possible. If you can manage to do that, your visitor will leave a good impression on your website and you have laid the groundwork for a good website.

3. Learnability

    Learnability is another important aspect of usability.
    It should be your goal to design intuitive interfaces — interfaces that don’t require instructions. Key to intuitive design is to make use of what people already know, or create something new that is easy to learn.
    If you use new concepts in your design, make sure to use them consistently and give people a hand during the initial learning phase. People like theose sites that are clean and easy to navigate.

4. Credibility

    Even if people are able to find what they came for, if they don't trust you, all of your effort is worthless. Therefore, it is crucial to let people know that you are a real company with real stuff working there. You can offer a clear "About Us" page to provide contact details and company description to visitors.

5. Relevancy

    It is not enough that your website is clear, your content must also be relevant. In other words, you must know who the users are and why they visit your website.
    First of all, define who your users are. Second, communicate with them to know what their goals are. Third, create user scenarios that illustrate in which situation people visit your site to find what kind of content.
